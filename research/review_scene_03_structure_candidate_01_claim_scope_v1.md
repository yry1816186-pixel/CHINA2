## A. Claims that are currently acceptable for controlled use
These are the claims that appear supportable enough for controlled scene-level use, subject to final master-table review:

1. Foguang Temple East Hall is a historically important surviving Tang timber building.
2. The East Hall can serve as a serious candidate anchor direction for discussing structural hierarchy in ancient Chinese timber architecture.
3. It is reasonable to use the East Hall as one reference point for explaining that structural organization, not surface decoration alone, is central to architectural reading.
4. It is reasonable to connect the candidate to discussion of girder-and-beam timber relations at a scene-level explanatory scale.

### Current handling recommendation
- treat these as `E3_supported` statements for scene-level explanatory anchoring
- keep them in explanatory language, not proof language
- keep them inside `C_abstract_explanatory_layer`

## B. Claims that may be usable but must stay narrow
These are claims that may be usable, but only with tighter wording and no broad extrapolation:

1. The East Hall is among the most important surviving Tang timber structures.
2. The East Hall demonstrates sophisticated girder-and-beam timber techniques.
3. Structural study material resembling the East Hall’s straight tenon joint style may support limited structural relation discussion.

### Current handling recommendation
- do not broaden these into totalizing claims about all Chinese ancient timber structures
- do not treat them as sufficient support for every bracket-set, load-path, or construction-stage claim
- keep them tied to one clearly bounded explanatory purpose

## C. Claims that should NOT be used yet
These are claims that should remain out of downstream scene use until stronger or more specific support is added:

1. Any claim that the scene proves a complete documentary reconstruction of the real East Hall.
2. Any claim that the East Hall alone proves the general structural logic of all Chinese ancient architecture.
3. Any precise structural-detail claim that goes beyond the current public-source bundle.
4. Any implication that a visually similar generated image is evidence of the real site.
5. Any claim that collapses explanatory abstraction into real-site continuity.

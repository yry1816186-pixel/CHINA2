## A. Claims that are currently acceptable for controlled use
These are the claims that appear supportable enough for controlled scene-level use, subject to final master-table review:

1. The Imperial Palace in Beijing is a historically important palace complex with unusually legible architectural hierarchy.
2. It can serve as a serious candidate anchor direction for explaining that order and threshold can be read through spatial organization.
3. It is reasonable to use the candidate as one reference point for discussing sequence, hierarchy and directional order in a bounded explanatory scene.

### Current handling recommendation
- treat these as `E3_supported` statements for scene-level orientation anchoring
- keep them in explanatory language, not proof language
- keep them inside `B_composite_teaching_scene`

## B. Claims that may be usable but must stay narrow
These are claims that may be usable, but only with tighter wording and no broad extrapolation:

1. The complex functioned as the ceremonial and political centre of the Chinese government.
2. The ensemble reflects important features in the development of Chinese palatial architecture.
3. Spatial sequence within the complex may support bounded discussion of hierarchy and threshold progression.

### Current handling recommendation
- do not broaden these into all-purpose claims about all Chinese spatial order traditions
- do not let them justify ceremonial-route, imperial-power or performative narrative framing
- keep them tied to one clearly bounded explanatory purpose: order and threshold, not authority spectacle

## C. Claims that should NOT be used yet
These are claims that should remain out of downstream scene use until stronger or more specific support is added:

1. Any claim that the scene proves a complete real-site route through the Forbidden City.
2. Any claim that the candidate alone proves all Chinese axial or hierarchical logic.
3. Any implication that generated imagery of a palace axis is evidence of historical use patterns.
4. Any claim that shifts the scene into imperial ceremony, political theatre or documentary reconstruction.
5. Any claim that lets monumentality stand in for evidence.

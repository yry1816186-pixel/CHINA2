## Why this case is a serious fit
- Public UNESCO material identifies the Imperial Palace as the seat of supreme power for more than five centuries.
- UNESCO also describes it as the ceremonial and political centre of the Chinese government and as an ensemble that reflects important features of Chinese palatial architecture.
- For the current scene, this makes it a strong candidate for explaining that order, threshold, hierarchy and spatial sequence are organized architecturally, not merely decorated visually.

## What this candidate could support
### Stronger support
- scene-level anchor for discussing directional order, hierarchical sequencing and threshold progression
- support for explaining that architectural order can be read through spatial organization at a large complex scale
- support for using one highly legible palace complex as a bounded orientation anchor in an explanatory scene

### Likely limited support
- it should not automatically support every claim about all Chinese spatial order traditions
- it should not automatically justify ceremonial, imperial, or power-route storytelling
- it should not automatically justify a documentary-style real-site reconstruction look
